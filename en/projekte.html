Master's Thesis: Artificial Intelligence and Computer Vision for the Automated Recognition of Assembly Steps in the Context of Assistance Systems

Objective

The project aims to develop a worker assistance system (WAS) for assembly that utilizes AI methods like computer vision and speech recognition to improve the manual assembly process. The motivation behind this project lies in the growing use of such assistance systems in the industry to support employees during complex and error-prone assembly steps. Many of these systems are still cumbersome in authorization and adaptation to different contexts. Therefore, the goal of this work is to implement an automated recognition of assembly steps to guide employees through the assembly process in real-time while minimizing errors. This should be realized through the recognition of objects, hand movements, and spoken language to eventually create assistance steps or automatically verify their correctness. The system was developed and tested under real conditions at the Unilokk assembly station of the Chair of Production Systems at Ruhr University Bochum.

Methodology and Implementation

The implementation of the work relies on methods of computer vision and speech recognition, which were trained on a specially created dataset. The YOLO object detection model, optimized for real-time applications, was used as the base model. Additionally, MediaPipe was employed for hand pose estimation to recognize the position and movement of hands in the assembly. Speech recognition algorithms were used to transcribe spoken instructions and integrate them into the assembly process. The development of the system was carried out using a modular approach to ensure flexibility and expandability. Special attention was paid to the integration of the various subsystems so that both visual and linguistic information could be processed and combined into a user-friendly interface.

Results and Evaluation

The developed assistance system was tested and evaluated by multiple independent individuals in a real assembly station. The results show that the system has a high recognition accuracy in object detection and efficient hand movement analysis. In particular, a significant reduction in the error rate was observed during error detection in assembly processes. Speech recognition proved useful for documenting assembly steps and supporting the training of new employees. However, the evaluation also showed that the system still has potential for improvement in terms of adaptability to different assembly situations. Especially the transferability of the system to more complex assembly situations and the integration of additional sensors and AI methods were identified as future research approaches.

Bachelor's Thesis: Development of a Mobile Collaborative Robotic System for Foaming Processes in the Automotive Industry

Objective

To increase flexibility and support on-site personnel, it is planned to automate the foaming process for the production of grommets. This is to be achieved using a robotic system that replaces the manual placement of the mixing head on the tool opening. The special feature of this robotic system is that it should independently recognize the positions of the tools and their openings instead of approaching predefined positions. This increases flexibility, as the arrangement and number of foaming systems and tools in the worker halls often change. The robotic system must function without manual reprogramming in new environments and be integrable into existing systems. It should be able to determine two positions for each tool: the location of the tool and the exact position of the opening. Since the tools have different sizes and shapes, the positions of the openings vary in X, Y, and Z directions. The robotic system must also function in real industrial environments.

Prototype

With the successful completion of the bachelor's thesis, a complete prototype was realized—a mobile manipulator consisting of two subsystems. This prototype was independently developed and is characterized by careful conception and design aimed at meeting specific requirements. The development process included designing in various domains such as mechanics, electronics, and computer science. Following these domain-specific designs, system integration took place, where the individual domains were combined into a functioning overall system. A crucial step was the validation and verification of the prototype to check whether all requirements were met and to identify areas that might still need work. The development, design, and construction of the prototype were carried out independently.

Interface Diagram

The interface diagram illustrates the composition and interactions between the two subsystems of the overall system. Subsystem 1 includes a chassis equipped with a variety of sensors that enable autonomous driving. A central sensor is the lane guidance sensor, which recognizes specially marked lanes identified by fluorescent tapes on the floor. Additionally, an RFID device captures the position of the tools, allowing the vehicle to position itself accordingly. Movement is provided by two motors controlled via H-bridges. The heart of this subsystem is a Controllino PLC control unit programmed with C/C++. Subsystem 2 includes a collaborative robot, the UR3, equipped with necessary sensors such as a camera and a distance sensor. These sensors allow the robot to precisely locate and target the holes of the tools to be filled. The interface diagram shows how these two subsystems interact to ensure seamless and efficient operation. It illustrates the data flows and control commands between the chassis and the robot, with particular emphasis on the integration of different sensor technologies and control units.
